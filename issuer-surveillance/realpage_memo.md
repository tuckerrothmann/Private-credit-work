# RealPage — issuer memo

## One-line thesis
RealPage is a special-situation software credit where **regulatory and litigation overhang** can impair enterprise value and buyer appetite before classic software-credit marks fully catch up.

## What the company does
RealPage sells software and data tools to property managers, landlords, and real-estate operators. Its products span property management, leasing, renter services, and revenue-management / pricing tools. It is financeable on paper because the software can look:
- deeply embedded in customer workflows
- operationally important to landlords and property managers
- recurring-revenue and data-enabled
- scalable inside a vertical with real switching friction

That same embeddedness becomes more fragile if legal or regulatory action attacks one of the company's core value propositions.

## Why it matters
- large Thoma Bravo-backed software take-private
- meaningful first-lien / second-lien debt structure publicly referenced
- legal / antitrust overhang linked to rent-setting software
- valuation damage can arrive through buyer-universe shrinkage and headline risk before operating collapse shows up cleanly

## Debt / financing timeline
- **December 2020:** Thoma Bravo announces an agreement to acquire RealPage in a transaction valued at approximately **$10.2 billion including net debt**.
- **April 2021:** RealPage says the acquisition closes and again cites the approximately **$10.2 billion** transaction value.
- **April 2021:** Latham says it advised a syndicate of international investment banks on a **$4.0 billion** financing package including a **first-lien term loan, revolving credit facility, and second-lien term facility**.
- **2022-2026:** RealPage becomes increasingly important as a surveillance case because legal and antitrust scrutiny can damage value even if the underlying software business initially appears stable.

## Capital-structure relevance
RealPage matters less as a classic "software growth faded" case and more as a reminder that software credits can be hit by **non-operating value destruction**.

In other words, a borrower can still have:
- recurring revenue
- decent customer entrenchment
- software-like margins

and still become a weaker credit if legal pressure changes:
- buyer appetite
- valuation confidence
- strategic flexibility
- refinancing willingness

## Hidden-mark-risk thesis
Regulatory and litigation pressure can erode enterprise value **gradually and unevenly**, leaving lender marks looking calm even as the buyer universe narrows and recovery confidence weakens.

## Why it could matter to BDC unsecured bonds
RealPage matters for bond investors because it is a good reminder that marks can be stale for reasons other than simple operating underperformance. If lender books contain credits with similar legal or regulatory clouds, investors may underestimate how quickly enterprise value can compress once a market-clearing event arrives.

## First transmission channel to BDC unsecured bond risk
The first transmission channel is likely **valuation-confidence erosion**, via:
1. weaker recovery assumptions as legal overhang deepens
2. softer marks or more cautious underwriting on similar vertical-software credits
3. lower confidence in portfolio carrying values where regulatory risk is underappreciated
4. eventual wider BDC unsecured bond spreads if investors conclude lender marks are slow to reflect special-situation risk

## What to watch
- evolution of DOJ / antitrust / state actions
- litigation milestones and remedies risk
- amendments, repricings, or maturity-management moves
- whether legal pressure appears to shrink buyer appetite or refinancing flexibility
- any sponsor actions to preserve or reposition value

## What would falsify or weaken the bearish view
The RealPage concern would ease if we saw:
- legal and regulatory pressure resolving without meaningful business-model damage
- evidence that customer retention and pricing power remain intact
- financing flexibility that suggests lenders still view the credit as fundamentally sound
- public signals that buyer appetite and recovery assumptions remain strong despite the overhang

## Working judgment
- Hidden mark risk: **4 / 5**
- BDC bond relevance: **3 / 5**
- Recovery fragility: **4 / 5**
