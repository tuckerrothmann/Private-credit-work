# Kaseya — issuer memo

## One-line thesis
Kaseya is a strong test of whether lenders are underestimating downside in **sticky-on-paper software platforms** where integration complexity, customer pushback, and refinancing structure can matter more than the recurring-revenue label suggests.

## What the company does
Kaseya provides IT-management, endpoint, security, backup, and automation software used heavily by managed service providers and internal IT teams. Lenders can like this profile because it appears to offer:
- deeply embedded operational workflows
- painful switching costs
- recurring software revenue
- infrastructure-like positioning rather than discretionary software spend
- consolidation opportunities under sponsor ownership

That is exactly why it is useful as a surveillance name. If this type of credit weakens, it suggests that private-credit marks may be too generous across a broader set of supposedly resilient software assets.

## Why it matters
- large Datto-related acquisition financing
- sponsor-backed software platform with integration complexity
- meaningful private-credit lender group visible in public sources
- refinancing activity can reveal whether the original private-credit structure was more aggressive than it appeared

## Debt / financing timeline
- **June 2022:** Kaseya announces and completes the **$6.2 billion** acquisition of Datto.
- **2022:** Paul, Weiss says the debt financing included **$3.7 billion of senior secured commitments**: a **$3.3 billion initial term loan**, **$200 million revolving credit facility**, and **$200 million delayed-draw term loan**, plus **$1 billion of preferred equity** at an indirect parent.
- **2022:** Paul, Weiss identifies notable financing sources / investors including **Golub Capital, Blackstone Credit, Ares, Owl Rock, Oak Hill, Carlyle Global Credit, and Sixth Street**.
- **2025:** public reporting summarized in project research indicates a refinancing process of roughly **$4 billion**, including a meaningful first-lien / second-lien structure.

## Capital-structure relevance
Kaseya is useful because the story sits right in the zone where private-credit underwriting often sounds most comfortable:
- sticky product set
- mission-critical use cases
- recurring revenue
- sponsor-backed scale
- clear acquisition logic

If that kind of borrower still needs a more defensive refinancing or reveals weaker-than-expected economics, the implication is that software credit risk is broader than obviously troubled categories.

## Hidden-mark-risk thesis
The likely blind spot is that a "sticky software" story can hide **integration strain, customer dissatisfaction, and slower deleveraging** until refinancing structure forces lenders to show what they really think.

## Why it could matter to BDC unsecured bonds
For unsecured BDC bond investors, Kaseya matters as a **confidence transmission case** more than a one-name impairment story. If lenders are forced to refinance at weaker economics, accept layered structure, or leave difficult risk behind, it undermines the idea that software-service platforms are inherently safe first-lien assets.

## First transmission channel to BDC unsecured bond risk
The likely first transmission channel is **underwriting-confidence erosion**, via:
1. refinancing terms that reveal weaker market appetite than expected
2. pressure on marks for directly or ecosystem-exposed lenders
3. skepticism toward other consolidated software-platform credits
4. spread widening in BDC unsecured bonds if investors infer that software-portfolio marks are flattered by lagging recognition

## What to watch
- exact refinancing structure and pricing
- first-lien / second-lien split
- any residual private-credit hold positions after syndication
- evidence of weaker-than-expected deleveraging
- operating strain, integration fatigue, or customer pushback
- whether markets are refinancing the name because the credit is truly healthier or simply because loan windows reopened

## What would falsify or weaken the bearish view
The Kaseya concern would soften if we saw:
- clean refinancing execution with strong demand
- limited need for structural complexity or expensive second-lien support
- evidence of stable customer retention and healthy cash generation
- clear deleveraging rather than reliance on favorable market windows

## Working judgment
- Hidden mark risk: **4 / 5**
- BDC bond relevance: **4 / 5**
- Recovery fragility: **4 / 5**
