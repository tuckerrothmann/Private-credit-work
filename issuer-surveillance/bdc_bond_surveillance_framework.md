# BDC Unsecured Bond Surveillance Framework

## Objective
Use underlying issuer surveillance to anticipate trouble for **BDC unsecured bonds** before it is fully visible in published marks, non-accruals, or headline NAV commentary.

This project is **not** mainly about predicting which software issuer defaults next in isolation. It is about finding issuer-level deterioration that could matter to:
- BDC asset marks
- future non-accrual migration
- spillover into NII / fee income / incentive fee dynamics
- confidence in lender underwriting quality
- spread widening or downside in BDC unsecured bonds

## Core question
For each issuer, ask:

**Could this name create lender pain that is currently under-recognized in marks, ratings commentary, or public BDC disclosure?**

## Why this matters for BDC unsecured bond investors
BDC unsecured bond holders sit above common equity but below the operating performance of the loan book.

That means the key risks are usually not isolated mark wiggles. They are:
1. **understated asset-quality deterioration**
2. **clusters of credits with similar hidden weakness**
3. **lagged recognition** in marks or accrual status
4. **erosion of confidence** in underwriting or sponsor behavior
5. **NAV / leverage pressure** that eventually changes how the unsecured bonds trade

In other words: a single issuer usually matters only if it is a signal, a concentration, or a catalyst.

## Issuer traits most relevant to bond risk
### 1) Mark-to-reality gap risk
We care most about names where:
- lender marks may still look benign
- public evidence suggests enterprise value or financing flexibility is worsening
- current pricing / carrying values may be slow to catch up

Examples of useful warning signs:
- secondary loan marks below about 90
- repeated quarter-over-quarter mark drift
- amendment chains without explicit mark recognition
- delayed recognition of accrual stress
- debt exchanges / LMEs / payment-flex changes that still leave bonds seemingly calm

### 2) Wrong-way liquidity or liability pressure
Even if the asset is still money-good, a lender platform can get into trouble if:
- liquidity needs rise while realizations slow
- interval-fund or evergreen liabilities create redemption pressure
- lenders need to support borrowers longer than planned
- financings that looked “temporary” become semi-permanent inventory

### 3) Software-specific recovery illusion
For software issuers, underwriting can overestimate downside protection because:
- ARR optics can mask weakening product relevance
- switching costs may be overstated
- hard collateral is limited
- recovery depends on franchise continuity, not asset coverage
- AI, pricing pressure, and budget scrutiny can shrink exit values faster than EBITDA narratives imply

### 4) Structural and sponsor behavior risk
Names deserve extra attention when there is evidence of:
- dividend recaps
- holdco layering
- second-lien / preferred overlays
- unrestricted-subsidiary moves
- sponsor/lender conflict
- litigation or regulatory overhang

## The best “dangerous issuer” profile for this project
The highest-value names tend to combine:
1. **credible private-credit lender exposure**
2. **public-source evidence of weakening fundamentals / refi risk / restructuring risk**
3. **asset-light or fragile recovery quality**
4. **some path for the stress to matter to visible BDCs or major private-credit platforms**
5. **a reasonable case that marks may still lag economic reality**

## Working scorecard for this lens
### A) Hidden mark risk (1-5)
How likely is it that current carrying values / public marks understate the real deterioration?
- 5 = strong risk of under-reflection
- 4 = meaningful risk
- 3 = mixed
- 2 = probably mostly recognized already
- 1 = little evidence of hidden mark risk

### B) BDC bond relevance (1-5)
How useful is this name for anticipating unsecured-bond trouble?
- 5 = high chance of informing credit views across visible BDCs / major platforms
- 4 = important lender-learning case
- 3 = useful but more idiosyncratic
- 2 = niche or weak visibility
- 1 = low transmission value

### C) Recovery fragility (1-5)
How weak could the lender recovery be in a real downside?
- 5 = very fragile / soft-asset / confidence-dependent
- 4 = fragile
- 3 = mixed
- 2 = somewhat supported
- 1 = relatively strong recovery setup

## What each issuer memo should answer
1. What is the business and why was it financeable?
2. Why might lenders be in more trouble than marks imply?
3. What are the clearest evidence points of weakening credit quality?
4. What is the likely lender / BDC transmission channel?
5. If stress deepens, what happens first: mark-down, amendment, PIK, non-accrual, or restructuring?
6. Why could recovery disappoint versus a standard recurring-revenue underwriting case?
7. What would make us less worried?

## Most important watch signals going forward
- price / mark drift versus prior quarter
- lender-led amendments or covenant loosening
- payment-flex / PIK features
- extensions without de-risking
- management turnover
- AI/product-pressure evidence in vulnerable software categories
- customer contraction or lower implied retention quality
- sponsor behavior that shifts value away from lenders
- evidence that visible BDCs are not yet reflecting the stress proportionately
