# Case Studies: Medallia and Pluralsight

## Why start with these two
They show two different but related failure modes in sponsor-backed software credit:
- **Medallia** = a large, modern software LBO with publicly identified direct lenders and subsequent mark-down concerns.
- **Pluralsight** = a more advanced distress / restructuring case where private-credit lenders effectively took control.

---

## Medallia

### What happened
- Medallia agreed to be acquired by **Thoma Bravo** in 2021 for **$6.4 billion**.
- Public financing coverage states that the deal used a **large recurring-revenue unitranche loan**.
- Latham publicly identified the financing as the **largest such structure to date** at the time and said it represented **Blackstone Alternative Credit Advisors as lead lender**, with Apollo, KKR credit arms, and Antares also involved.
- Subsequent public reporting in 2025-2026 pointed to **loan markdowns** and wide divergence in marks across holders, suggesting weakening credit confidence and uncertainty around true enterprise value under stress.

### Why it matters
- It is one of the clearest examples of how private credit embraced the software underwriting playbook: sticky revenue, recurring subscriptions, and sponsor operational upside.
- It also shows how quickly the thesis can weaken when growth slows, leadership changes hit confidence, or lenders reassess what recurring-revenue software is worth in a more skeptical market.
- Recovery quality is a central issue: Medallia is asset-light, and lender value likely depends far more on preserving customer base and software relevance than on any hard-asset collateral.

### What to look for in similar names
- Was underwriting based more on **ARR** than conventional EBITDA / cash interest coverage?
- Are there signs of **softening growth, retention slippage, or execution churn**?
- Do marks differ widely across lenders, suggesting weak price discovery?
- Is the product mission-critical enough to support an orderly sale if the sponsor steps back?

### Initial credit lesson
Software loans can look senior-secured on paper but still behave like **highly valuation-sensitive credits** when the real collateral is customer stickiness and strategic relevance rather than hard assets.

## Pluralsight

### What happened
- Vista took **Pluralsight** private in 2021 in a deal reportedly funded with **over $1.5 billion of debt**, including a **$1.175 billion recurring-revenue loan** and a revolver.
- By 2024, public reporting described a much weaker picture: equity marked to zero by Vista, operating headwinds, rising unhedged interest costs, workforce changes, and a sponsor-supported liability management transaction involving IP movement to a subsidiary.
- Goodwin summarized reporting that a lender group including **Blue Owl, Ares, Golub, Oaktree, Benefit Street, Goldman Sachs, and BlackRock** would take ownership through a debt-for-equity exchange.
- KBRA called Pluralsight an important **bellwether** and noted exposure across rated BDCs and other private credit vehicles.

### Why it matters
- This is a rare, highly visible example of a **large-cap private-credit software restructuring**.
- It demonstrates that recurring revenue and sponsor ownership do not prevent distress when leverage is high and interest burden rises.
- It also shows the emerging playbook for software workouts in private credit: out-of-court solutions, liability management exercises, lender cooperation, sponsor optionality, and eventual lender ownership.

### What to look for in similar names
- Does the borrower need **interim sponsor capital** just to make interest payments?
- Are assets being moved to subsidiaries or other entities to create new financing capacity?
- Are lenders coordinating early, or are they fragmented?
- Does the company still have a defendable operating franchise after the balance sheet is reset?

### Initial credit lesson
Pluralsight is the strongest public cautionary tale that **private-credit software stress can migrate from mark-downs to control transfer** faster than the old “sticky SaaS” narrative would suggest.

## Comparison summary

| Topic | Medallia | Pluralsight |
|---|---|---|
| Stage of credit story | Stress / mark-down / watchlist | Full restructuring / lender takeover |
| Public lender visibility | Strong | Strong |
| Key issue | Underperformance and valuation sensitivity | Underperformance plus capital-structure failure |
| Recovery question | What is the real enterprise value under stress? | How much value survives after lenders take the keys? |
| Use in this project | Current watchlist anchor | Historical downside / workout anchor |

## Bottom line
If Tucker wants a fast mental model for the whole project, it is this:
- **Medallia** = what a software private-credit problem looks like **before** the final workout.
- **Pluralsight** = what it looks like **after the workout logic starts to win.**
